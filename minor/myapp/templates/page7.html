Lichen Planus

A comprehensive guide to understanding this inflammatory condition that can affect skin, hair, nails, and mucous membranes.

Common Symptoms on the Scalp

Purple-Red Bumps

Small, flat-topped, purplish or reddish bumps (papules) that are often shiny and itchy.

Intense Itching

The affected areas on the scalp can be extremely itchy, leading to discomfort and scratching.

Patchy Hair Loss

Inflammation around hair follicles can lead to scarring and permanent, patchy hair loss (cicatricial alopecia).

Scalp Redness & Scaling

The skin around the hair follicles may become red, swollen, and covered with fine scales.

Treatment Options

Topical Corticosteroids

High-potency steroid creams, lotions, or foams applied directly to the scalp to reduce inflammation and itching.

Steroid Injections

Injecting corticosteroids directly into the inflamed lesions on the scalp for targeted treatment.

Oral Medications

Systemic drugs like retinoids, immunosuppressants, or antimalarials for widespread or severe cases.

Light Therapy (Phototherapy)

Controlled exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light can help clear up the skin lesions in some cases.

Management Tips

Avoid Scratching

Minimize scratching the itchy areas to prevent further skin trauma, which can worsen the condition.

Use Gentle Hair Care

Use mild, fragrance-free shampoos and avoid harsh chemical treatments or tight hairstyles.

Manage Stress

Practice stress-reduction techniques like meditation, as stress can trigger or exacerbate flare-ups.

Detailed Information

What is Lichen Planus?

Autoimmune Condition: It's believed to be an autoimmune disease where the immune system mistakenly attacks cells of the skin or mucous membranes.

Not Contagious: Lichen planus is not an infection and cannot be spread from person to person.

Lichen Planopilaris: When it affects the scalp and causes hair loss, it is specifically called Lichen Planopilaris.

Potential Triggers

Medications: Certain drugs, including some for high blood pressure, heart disease, and arthritis, can trigger it.

Infections: Viral infections like Hepatitis C have been linked to an increased risk.

Stress: Severe emotional stress can precede or worsen an outbreak.

Allergens: Contact with allergens, like certain metals in dental fillings, can sometimes be a cause.
