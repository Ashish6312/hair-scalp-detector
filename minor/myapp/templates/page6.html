Head Lice

Primary Treatment Methods

Common Symptoms

Intense Scalp Itching

Severe itching caused by an allergic reaction to lice bites, often worse behind the ears and at the neck.

Visible Lice or Nits

Seeing adult lice crawling in hair or finding tiny white/yellow nits (eggs) firmly attached to hair shafts.

Red Bumps and Sores

Small red bumps on the scalp, neck, and shoulders, which may become infected from scratching.

Difficulty Sleeping

Irritability and trouble sleeping due to intense itching, as lice are most active in the darkness.

Treatment Options

Medicated Shampoos

Over-the-counter or prescription lotions (Permethrin, Ivermectin) that kill live lice and nits.

Wet-Combing

Using a fine-toothed nit comb on wet, conditioned hair to physically remove lice and their eggs.

Oral Medications

Prescription pills like oral ivermectin may be used for difficult or resistant cases of lice.

Environmental Cleaning

Washing all bedding, clothing, and personal items in hot water to eliminate any stray lice.

Prevention Tips

Avoid Head-to-Head Contact

Minimize direct head contact during play, sports, and other close-contact activities in schools.

Don't Share Personal Items

Avoid sharing combs, brushes, hats, scarves, headphones, or any other items that touch the head.

Regular Scalp Checks

Perform weekly checks on children, especially during school outbreaks, to catch infestations early.

Prompt Treatment

Treat any infestation immediately and notify close contacts to prevent further spread.

Detailed Information

The Lice Life Cycle

Nits (Eggs): Tiny oval eggs that are firmly attached to hair shafts and hatch in 7-10 days.

Nymphs: Immature lice that hatch from nits and mature into adults in about 7-10 days.

Adults: Fully grown lice, about the size of a sesame seed, which can live for up to 30 days on a human host.

Common Risk Factors

Age: Most common in preschool and elementary school-aged children (ages 3-11).

Close Contact: Spreads easily in environments where children play closely, like schools and daycare.

Gender: Girls are more frequently affected than boys, possibly due to longer hair and social contact.

Household: Spreads easily to family members of an infected individual.
