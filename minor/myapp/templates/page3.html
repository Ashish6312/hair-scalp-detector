Common Symptoms

Patchy Hair Loss

Sudden appearance of round or oval bald patches on the scalp, which are typically smooth.

Scalp Visibility

Increased visibility of the scalp through thinning hair, especially noticeable in bright lighting.

Sudden Shedding

Rapid and unexpected loss of hair in clumps, often found on pillows or in the shower.

Facial Hair Loss

Loss of hair from eyebrows, eyelashes, and beards can occur alongside scalp hair loss.

Nail Changes

Small dents, ridges, or white spots on fingernails and toenails can accompany alopecia.

Treatment Options

Corticosteroid Injections

Direct injections into bald patches to reduce inflammation and stimulate hair regrowth.

Topical Minoxidil

An FDA-approved solution applied to the scalp to promote hair growth and prevent further loss.

Hair Transplant

Surgical procedure to transplant healthy hair follicles to bald patches for permanent restoration.

Immunotherapy

Using chemicals to trigger an immune response that can stimulate new hair growth.

JAK Inhibitors

Newer oral medications that block enzymes involved in the autoimmune response causing hair loss.

Prevention & Management

Reduce Stress

Practice stress management like meditation and yoga, as stress can be a trigger.

Early Consultation

Seek professional medical advice at the first signs of unusual hair loss for early intervention.

Avoid Tight Hairstyles

Prevent traction alopecia by avoiding styles that pull on hair follicles for long periods.

Healthy Diet

Consume a balanced diet rich in vitamins, minerals, and proteins essential for hair health.

Gentle Hair Care

Use mild shampoos and avoid harsh chemical treatments that can damage hair follicles.

Detailed Information

Types of Alopecia

Alopecia Areata: Patchy hair loss in round shapes, typically on the scalp.

Alopecia Totalis: Complete loss of all hair on the scalp.

Alopecia Universalis: Total loss of hair on the entire scalp and body.

Androgenetic Alopecia: Male or female pattern baldness, the most common type of hair loss.

Common Risk Factors

Autoimmune Conditions: Having other autoimmune diseases increases the risk.

Family History: A genetic predisposition plays a significant role in many types of alopecia.

Severe Stress: Physical or emotional trauma can trigger sudden hair loss in susceptible individuals.

Hormonal Changes: Events like pregnancy, menopause, and thyroid disorders can contribute.
